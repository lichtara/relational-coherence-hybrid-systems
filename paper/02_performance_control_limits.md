# 02 — Performance, Control, and the Limits of Fragmented Governance

Debates on AI governance have unfolded across distinct intellectual trajectories. Sociotechnical traditions emphasize the co-production of technology and social order, the relational constitution of artifacts, and the situated character of human–machine interaction. Alignment and control research frames governance as the problem of ensuring that artificial systems pursue specified goals. Normative and accountability scholarship foregrounds responsibility, contestability, and institutional design.

Each tradition captures a necessary dimension of governance. Yet they operate in structural isolation.

Interaction-centered approaches illuminate relational processes but rarely theorize the conditions under which those relations remain intelligible and governable across time. Alignment frameworks address behavioral convergence but often abstract from the relational architecture within which authority must remain locatable and accountable. Regulatory models articulate boundaries of responsibility, yet frequently presuppose stable fields and coherent dynamics that hybrid systems actively destabilize.

The shared limitation is not theoretical inadequacy but structural fragmentation. Interaction, optimization, and boundary-setting are treated as partially separable problems. What remains insufficiently articulated is the minimal structural architecture within which relational intelligibility, temporal compatibility, and differentiated authority must co-exist for governability to persist.

The triadic model advanced here addresses this gap not by adding another framework, but by articulating the structural conditions presupposed by all of them.

## 02.1 Canonical Terminology of the Triadic Model

The structural framework developed in this article will be referred to as the **Triadic Theory of Governability (TTG)**.

Within TTG:

- **Field** designates the structured relational horizon within which agency and authority become locatable.
- **Coherence** designates the temporal compatibility of systemic dynamics across heterogeneous layers.
- **Limit** designates the differentiated boundary conditions that render authority attributable and intervention possible.

These are not policy instruments or normative aspirations. They are irreducible structural conditions of possibility for governability under distributed and hybridized agency.

The remainder of the article employs this terminology in its strict structural sense.

# Problem Statement

The rapid integration of artificial intelligence into institutional and societal systems has exposed a widening divergence between performance and governability.

Hybrid systems frequently operate with high levels of technical efficiency. They generate accurate outputs, scale decision-making, and optimize processes across domains. Yet such systems may simultaneously become increasingly opaque, less contestable, and progressively incapable of sustaining accountable authority.

The problem is not malfunction. It is structural drift.

Performance measures output quality. Governability concerns the conditions under which power remains intelligible, attributable, and revisable. A system may coordinate effectively and comply procedurally while losing the relational clarity and differentiated authority that render governance possible in the first place.

Existing governance approaches tend to privilege one structural dimension at the expense of others.

Interaction-centered models examine localized exchanges but often presuppose a stable relational environment. They seldom account for the possibility that the Field itself may lose intelligibility over time.

Optimization-driven approaches prioritize alignment and convergence. Yet alignment of outputs does not ensure compatibility across temporal sequences or differentiation of responsibility. Coherence conceived as convergence may conceal cumulative contradiction.

Regulatory and compliance frameworks articulate limits. Yet boundaries detached from relational intelligibility and temporal compatibility risk becoming symbolic or destabilizing. Formal constraints cannot compensate for incoherent dynamics or opaque fields.

Across these approaches, fragmentation obscures the structural conditions under which governability deteriorates. Hybrid systems may satisfy performance benchmarks and regulatory requirements while progressively eroding the architecture that makes authority accountable.

The problem addressed in this article is therefore structural rather than instrumental. It concerns the minimal architecture required for governability to exist at all under conditions of distributed and hybridized agency.

Governability, as argued here, presupposes three irreducible conditions:

- a structured relational Field within which agency is locatable,
- a temporally sustainable Coherence across systemic dynamics,
- and differentiated Limits that render authority attributable.

Remove any one, and what remains may be operation, control, or compliance—but not governability.

The triadic model developed in the following sections does not offer an additional governance framework. It articulates the structural grammar within which governance frameworks themselves must operate if power is to remain political rather than merely effective.
